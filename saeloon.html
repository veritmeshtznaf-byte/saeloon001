Saeloon

A fictional micronation-style online community (2020–2024)

العربية

Saeloon was a fictional micronation-style identity and online community that originated from a private Facebook Messenger group founded in 2020. Saeloon did not represent a real country or political entity; instead, it functioned as a symbolic framework through which members expressed group belonging, shared culture, and collective identity.

Background

Saeloon was founded in 2020 by Mohamed Khaled together with a small circle of friends, including Ziad al 7mir. The group began as a casual social space for sharing memes, informal conversation, and discussion of everyday topics such as health, personal life, and media. In its early phase the chat had no fixed name and titles changed frequently for humorous effect.

Name and identity

In early 2021 Mohamed Khaled proposed establishing a stable group identity to strengthen members' sense of belonging. The adopted name, "Saeloon", served as a symbolic designation for the collective and provided continuity across recurring group themes and inside jokes.

Symbolism and flag

As part of the identity-building process, Mohamed Khaled designed a black-and-white skull flag using image-editing software, inspired by traditional pirate iconography (Jolly Roger). The flag was intended as an internal emblem rather than a political or national symbol. When the original image was not available publicly, participants sometimes used generic pirate-style imagery as a visual substitute.

Organization and membership

Saeloon had no formal governance, written constitution, or recognized territory. Leadership and status were informal and based on participation, contribution, and social influence within the chat. Membership fluctuated over time, with members joining and leaving; a smaller group of long-standing participants later came to be regarded informally as the founders of the Saeloon identity.

Affiliations

Saeloon had links to two distinct online groups that operated alongside or adjacent to the Saeloon chat; accounts of these relationships are based on participant recollections.

  • SIMP / WHYTBS — A separate group chat sometimes referred to as WHYTBS ("We Help You To Be a Simp"). Mohamed Khaled and Mohamed Ayman were formerly associated, and Kahk was among the founders of SIMP. The SIMP affiliation is therefore described as a former and separate association rather than part of Saeloon's core activities. [1]
  • صالون فوزي الثقافي (Salon Fawzi Cultural) — An informal cultural circle led by Ahmed FwZzy. Participant accounts report he engaged frequently with Mohamed Khaled. Sometimes described as a "twin" association to Saeloon; a migration/merger initiative failed due to interpersonal conflicts. [1]

Incidents

Ahmalid

Ahmalid was the youngest participant in Saeloon. He left the group after a conflict that took place on external platforms. According to participant accounts (quoted verbatim):

Ahmalid said to Kahk: "Since when you are my friend?"

Kahk explained that it was his fault and apologized. Ahmalid replied: "Your apology is useless to me." Kahk said "Ok," and subsequently blocked Ahmalid from all Saeloon-related apps. Participant recollections further note that Ahmalid followed certain atheist Egyptian figures on social media (for example, Sherif Gaber and Kosay Betar), which some members interpreted as influence from external Discord servers and communities. Mazen

According to participant accounts, a prolonged security and interpersonal dispute involving the founder, Mohamed Khaled (Kahk), and an individual identified by participants as Mazen Mohamed of Port Said occurred during 2020–2022. The following summary is based on participant testimony and archived chat excerpts; specific factual claims remain unverified.

In mid-2020 Kahk entered a period described by participants as his "memer prime", during which he created and circulated memes on a newly created Facebook profile. He later became acquainted with a user known to participants as Mazen Mohamed, who was described in-group as a prankster with some technical knowledge.

Kahk and Mazen reportedly co-founded a Facebook group called "INFO • SCP", intended for SCP and creepypasta content. The group grew rapidly; participants recall it reached roughly 300 members within about a month.

Participants state that Mazen repeatedly engaged in pranks and social engineering attempts. At one point he allegedly asked Kahk for his password (offering his own in return), and Kahk reportedly granted access.

According to participant testimony, approximately one year later the group's administration pages were banned from Facebook after a series of reports. Participants attribute the reports to Mazen, which led to the group and certain admin pages being removed; this caused a major rift between Kahk and Mazen and prompted Kahk to block Mazen across platforms.

Participant accounts further allege that Mazen later caused physical damage to Kahk's phone by burning its motherboard; Kahk only became aware of the loss of device functionality after some months, and later confirmed the event when he accessed account email records from another device. These assertions are presented here as reported by participants and have not been independently corroborated.

After the incident Kahk created a new Facebook account under the name "Zuck Bug" and implemented additional account protections (email-only registration, exclusion of phone numbers, and other security measures). Participants report that Mazen eventually ceased contact and the conflict subsided, allowing Kahk to continue managing Saeloon activities.

Influences & internal change

Members cited a variety of internet cultural sources and alternative-media aesthetics as influences on the group's style and content; the work of musician/visual artist Jack Stauber was specifically mentioned by participants. Internal changes in members' personal lives, including Saged Khaled's reported religious reorientation and stated decision to withdraw from certain group behaviours, are reported by participants as contributing factors to shifts in group dynamics. According to participant testimony, Saged informed Kahk that he had "changed his mentality" and intended to leave certain behaviours behind; Kahk subsequently agreed and the founders began to wind down public group activity, moving some communication to smaller private chats (notably Sanitarium).

Dissolution

Following the public closure, some former members continued limited interaction via private chats and one-on-one communication; a private group named Sanitarium reportedly included Mohamed Ayman and Saged Khaled.
